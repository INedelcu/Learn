Hemisphere sampling: uniform vs cosine-weighted

08_hemisphere_sampling.cpp — where the series meets the path tracer. The rendering equation is a hemisphere integral with a cosine factor; sampling directions proportional to that cosine cancels it and slashes the noise.

A surface point gathers light from every direction in the hemisphere above it, weighted by $\cos\theta$ (Lambert’s law — light arriving at a grazing angle is spread thin). We estimate that integral by sampling directions $\omega$ from a density $p(\omega)$ and averaging $f_r L_i\cos\theta / p(\omega)$. The choice of $p$ is the importance-sampling lever from example 06, now in its most important guise: matching $p$ to the $\cos\theta$ in the integrand.

One-sentence summary. Sampling the hemisphere cosine-weighted ($p=\cos\theta/\pi$) instead of uniformly ($p=1/2\pi$) makes the $\cos\theta$ factor cancel in the estimator $f_r L_i\cos\theta/p$ — for the pure cosine integral that cancellation is total (every sample returns the exact answer, zero variance), and for diffuse bounces it reduces the Lambertian weight to just the albedo.

1. The integral: light over the hemisphere

The outgoing radiance is the reflectance integral — the BRDF $f_r$ times incoming light $L_i$ times the cosine of the angle to the normal, integrated over the hemisphere $\mathcal H^2$:

$$L_o(\omega_o)=\int_{\mathcal H^2} f_r(\omega_o,\omega_i)\,L_i(\omega_i)\,\cos\theta_i\,d\omega_i\;\approx\;\frac1N\sum_{i=1}^N\frac{f_r\,L_i\,\cos\theta_i}{p(\omega_i)} .$$

With $\theta$ measured from the normal, $\cos\theta$ is just the $z$-component of the (unit) direction. The whole game on this page is choosing $p(\omega)$ well.

2. Two ways to sample a direction

Both strategies turn two uniform numbers $u_1,u_2$ into a unit direction $(x,y,z)$ with $z=\cos\theta$.

Uniform over solid angle — $p(\omega)=\dfrac{1}{2\pi}$

Every direction equally likely. Sampling $z$ uniformly in $[0,1]$ gives uniform solid angle (equal-area bands of the dome get equal probability):

$$z=u_1,\qquad r=\sqrt{1-z^2},\qquad \phi=2\pi u_2,\qquad \omega=(r\cos\phi,\ r\sin\phi,\ z).$$

Cosine-weighted — $p(\omega)=\dfrac{\cos\theta}{\pi}$

Directions near the normal are more likely, in proportion to $\cos\theta$. The slick way is Malley’s method: sample a point uniformly on the unit disk and lift it straight up onto the hemisphere.

$$r=\sqrt{u_1},\quad \phi=2\pi u_2,\quad (x,y)=(r\cos\phi,\ r\sin\phi),\quad z=\sqrt{1-u_1}.$$

4. Why cosine-weighting wins: the cosine cancels

This is the zero-variance ideal of example 06, realized. When the integrand contains a $\cos\theta$ and you sample with $p\propto\cos\theta$, that factor divides out of $f/p$. The file shows it on two integrals with known answers:

integralexactuniform estimator $f/p$cosine estimator $f/p$
$\displaystyle\int_{\mathcal H^2}\cos\theta\,d\omega$$\pi$$2\pi z$  (varies $0\!\to\!2\pi$)$\pi$ — constant!
$\displaystyle\int_{\mathcal H^2}\cos^3\theta\,d\omega$$\pi/2$$2\pi z^3$$\pi z^2$  (one cosine cancelled)

For integral (1), $f=\cos\theta=z$ and $p=z/\pi$, so $f/p=\pi$ for every sample — zero variance, the exact answer from a single draw. For integral (2), $f=z^3$ and $p=z/\pi$ give $f/p=\pi z^2$: one of the three cosines cancels, flattening the estimator. The per-sample variances are exact once you know how each sampler distributes $z=\cos\theta$ — uniform sets $z=u_1$, so $z\sim\mathrm{Uniform}[0,1]$; cosine sets $z=\sqrt{1-u_1}$, so $z^2\sim\mathrm{Uniform}[0,1]$, giving $z$ the density $2z$:

$$\begin{aligned} \textstyle\int\cos\theta\ \text{(uniform)}:\ &\operatorname{Var}[2\pi z]=4\pi^2\cdot\tfrac1{12}=\tfrac{\pi^2}{3}\approx3.29,\\ \textstyle\int\cos^3\theta\ \text{(uniform)}:\ &\operatorname{Var}[2\pi z^3]=4\pi^2\big(\tfrac17-\tfrac1{16}\big)=\tfrac{9\pi^2}{28}\approx3.17,\\ \textstyle\int\cos^3\theta\ \text{(cosine)}:\ &\operatorname{Var}[\pi z^2]=\tfrac{\pi^2}{3}-\tfrac{\pi^2}{4}=\tfrac{\pi^2}{12}\approx0.82. \end{aligned}$$

So cosine sampling zeroes integral (1) outright and cuts integral (2)'s variance by $\dfrac{9\pi^2/28}{\pi^2/12}=\dfrac{27}{7}\approx3.9\times$. The program confirms all four: measured per-sample variances $3.289$, $\sim\!10^{-32}$, $3.171$, and $0.823$, with each estimate on its exact value ($\pi$ and $\pi/2$).

5. The payoff, sample by sample

Histogram the per-sample estimator values $f(\omega)/p(\omega)$ themselves — the quantities being averaged. The tighter their spread, the lower the variance. Switch integrals and compare: for $\int\cos\theta$ the cosine estimator collapses to a single spike at $\pi$ (every sample is exactly the answer), while uniform spreads across the whole range.

6. Why Malley’s method gives the cosine

Sampling a uniform point on the disk and projecting it straight up onto the hemisphere produces cosine-weighted directions — no trig, no inverse CDF over $\theta$. The reason is a clean change of variables: a direction at angle $\theta$ projects to disk radius $r=\sin\theta$. A uniform disk has areal density $\tfrac1\pi$; converting the area element to solid angle introduces exactly a $\cos\theta$, so the solid-angle density comes out $p(\omega)=\cos\theta/\pi$.

Why a $\cos\theta$: orthographic projection foreshortens a solid-angle patch onto the disk by exactly the cosine, $dA=\cos\theta\,d\omega$. A point uniform in disk area has density $\tfrac1\pi$, so the induced solid-angle density is $p(\omega)=\tfrac1\pi\dfrac{dA}{d\omega}=\tfrac{\cos\theta}{\pi}$. You can also read it off the sampler: $z=\sqrt{1-u_1}$ makes $z^2$ uniform, so $z$ has density $2z$; with $\phi$ uniform and $d\omega=dz\,d\phi$, that is $p(\omega)=\tfrac{2z}{2\pi}=\tfrac{z}{\pi}=\tfrac{\cos\theta}{\pi}$.

7. This is the diffuse bounce in the renderer

Rendering link. A Lambertian (diffuse) surface has BRDF $f_r=\rho/\pi$ (albedo over $\pi$). Sampling the bounce direction cosine-weighted, $p=\cos\theta/\pi$, makes the weight $\dfrac{f_r\,L_i\,\cos\theta}{p}=\dfrac{(\rho/\pi)\,L_i\,\cos\theta}{\cos\theta/\pi}=\rho\,L_i$ — both the $\cos\theta$ and the $\pi$ cancel, leaving just the albedo. That is exactly why SampleDiffuseLambert returns a weight of plain albedo with no leftover cosine: the cosine-weighted pdf was chosen precisely to cancel it.

8. The code

The complete sample on GitHub: 08_hemisphere_sampling.cpp.

Each iteration draws one sample with each strategy and feeds the per-sample estimator value into an online mean/variance accumulator (Welford, example 01):

// uniform over solid angle:  p = 1/(2π),  z = cosθ uniform in [0,1]
double z = u1;  double pdf = 1.0/(2.0*PI);
uni1.add( z       / pdf );   // ∫cosθ   →  2π·z
uni2.add( z*z*z   / pdf );   // ∫cos³θ  →  2π·z³

// cosine-weighted (Malley):  p = cosθ/π,  z = sqrt(1 - u1)
double z = sqrt(1.0 - u1);  double pdf = z/PI;
cos1.add( z       / pdf );   // ∫cosθ   →  z/(z/π) = π        (constant → var 0)
cos2.add( z*z*z   / pdf );   // ∫cos³θ  →  z³/(z/π) = π·z²

printf("variance reduction (2): %.1fx\n", uni2.var()/cos2.var());  // ~3.9x

Integral (1)’s cosine column reports variance exactly zero (every value is $\pi$); integral (2)’s drops several-fold. Both strategies are unbiased — the means match $\pi$ and $\pi/2$.

9. Takeaways

  • The rendering equation is a hemisphere integral with a $\cos\theta$ factor. We estimate it as $\frac1N\sum f_r L_i\cos\theta/p(\omega)$; the art is choosing $p$.
  • Uniform solid angle ($p=1/2\pi$, sample $z=u_1$) wastes effort on grazing directions the cosine down-weights.
  • Cosine-weighted ($p=\cos\theta/\pi$, Malley’s disk-lift) puts samples where the cosine sends them, cancelling that factor in $f/p$.
  • Total cancellation for $\int\cos\theta$ — zero variance, the ideal importance-sampling case; ~3.9× for $\int\cos^3\theta$.
  • It is the diffuse bounce. Cosine-weighted sampling reduces the Lambertian weight to plain albedo — the principle behind SampleDiffuseLambert.
